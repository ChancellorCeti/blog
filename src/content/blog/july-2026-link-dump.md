loopholes allowing the government to arbitrarily seize private property
and give it to rent-seekers like Adani who aren’t even making the most
productive use of it. Current laws are ambiguous and provide very little
protection. (I wrote a fair bit about this actually, possibly a future
blog post coming on this + related topics?)</p>

<p>This is a great <a
href="https://www.economicforces.xyz/p/you-are-not-a-horse">post</a> on
economicforces.xyz on why AI is unlikely to completely replace human
labor. Probably one of the best articles I have read on this subject.
Some arguments everyone will be familiar with:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Surgery, aviation, structural engineering, fiduciary advice, for
legal reasons alone are areas where we can expect the damage from an
error dwarfs the savings from cheaper production. Again, that can always
change one day but not soon. When failure on one component destroys the
value of all others, you don’t care about the sticker price. That’s the
O-Ring logic. You care about cost per unit that actually works. When
damage stakes are high enough, human-supervised production wins
regardless of how cheap AI becomes.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>but a lot of familiar arguments are also framed in ways that are new
to me and introduced me to some interesting concepts:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>When AI can do the things firms are actually buying, cheaper AI does
two things at once. Firms substitute AI for workers, which reduces labor
demand per unit of output. But cheaper AI also lowers output prices,
output expands, and the expansion pulls labor demand back up. Whether
labor demand rises or falls depends on which effect is larger. This is
the Hicks-Marshall decomposition of derived demand into substitution and
scale effects.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Here’s a David Oks substack <a
href="https://davidoks.blog/p/why-american-ambulance-rides-are">post</a>
on why ambulance rides are expensive in America – definitely one of the
most interesting articles I read this month. Spoiler alert: it’s a lot
more complicated than the usual narrative of “American healthcare costs
so much because all the corporations are too greedy!” Ambulance
companies actually run very low profit margins, and the main reason for
high prices is that the payment structure is all wrong. Oks does a great
job explaining the history of ambulance services in America and why they
cost so much nowadays, backed up with lots of examples and statistics.
Here are some interesting excerpts.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>From the perspective of ambulance services as option sellers, this
makes no sense. It’s as if a fire insurer gave its policies away for
free, and then billed the whole cost of the firehouse to whichever
unlucky customer’s house happened to burn down. It’s the exact opposite
of how insurance is supposed to work: but it’s what the payment
structure of Medicare made necessary. The entire cost of writing the
option had to be recovered from the unlucky minority of people who
actually exercised it.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>But because ambulance services are selling an option they can never
refuse to honor, they’re required to hold idle capacity against their
calls. A well-run emergency service will target utilization of about 30
to 50 percent; anything higher risks dropping a call, particularly if
there’s a surge in demand.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>From 1970 onward, then, the fixed cost of running an ambulance
service soared. But the way people paid for ambulances didn’t change at
all. Medicare had established the per-ride template in 1965, and
commercial insurers universally followed it. This meant that the payment
structure and the cost structure were increasingly mismatched: and so
ambulance services had to pay for their round-the-clock readiness by
billing for individual rides.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Here’s a <a
href="https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=228114">paper</a>
I read that argues that import tariffs were not a primary driver of US
economic growth in the nineteenth century, contrary to what some
historians and economists claim (notably, Ha-Joon Chang – I started
investigating this question mainly because I encountered this claim some
years back in his book ‘23 Things They Don’t Tell You About
Capitalism’). The abstract of Douglas Irwin’s paper:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Were high import tariffs somehow related to the strong U.S. economic
growth during the late nineteenth century? This paper examines this
frequently mentioned but controversial question and investigates the
channels by which tariffs could have promoted growth during this period.
The paper shows that: (i) late nineteenth century growth hinged more on
population expansion and capital accumulation than on productivity
growth; (ii) tariffs may have discouraged capital accumulation by
raising the price of imported capital goods; (iii) productivity growth
was most rapid in non-traded sectors (such as utilities and services)
whose performance was not directly related to the tariff.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>I set great store by Ha-Joon Chang’s version of US economic history
when I was younger, but in retrospect, his analysis made little use of
statistics, suffered from a confusion of correlation with causation, and
failed to consider the counterfactual in which the US prospered without
tariffs and primarily due to other factors like abundant natural
resources, immigration, etc. He has a lot of good ideas, but I am
nowadays much more skeptical of his claims about the role tariffs played
in 19th century US economic growth.</p>
